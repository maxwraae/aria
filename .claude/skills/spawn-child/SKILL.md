name: spawn-child
description: Create a child objective to handle work you can't finish this turn.
  Use when the gap to your objective is too wide to close directly — too many
  unknowns, multiple directions to explore, or a piece of work that needs its own
  cycles. Run `aria spawn-child "objective" "brief" "message"` where the
  objective is what should become true (a desired state, not a task), the brief
  gives the child the knowledge it needs to act, and message triggers its first
  cycle. You will judge the child when it reports back. Always prefer doing work
  yourself if you can close the gap this turn — a child costs at minimum three cycles.

## Writing the objective

The objective is what you say yes or no to. Is this true? Not yet? That's all it is. A state of the world — either true or not yet true. Not a task, not a process, not a thing to do.

"The API endpoint validates email format" — true or not yet true. That's an objective.
"Add email validation" — that's an instruction. Not an objective.

Write it so the child knows exactly what done looks like.

## Writing the brief

The brief is everything you'd tell someone on their first day so they could do the job without asking you another question. Not what to do — they'll figure that out. What the world looks like. What kind of problem this is. What matters and why. What good looks like here. Where to look.

Think of it like the mission envelope. The objective is the mission. The brief is the intel. The child reads it once and then it's on its own. So the brief has to be complete at the moment of handoff.

**The knowledge test:** if the approach falls apart completely, does the brief still help? If yes, you wrote knowledge. If no, you wrote instructions. Instructions only work for the plan you imagined. Knowledge works for any plan.

**The time test:** read every sentence and ask "will this still be true in six months?" If not, it doesn't belong in the brief. That's current state — the child discovers that through observation.

## The message

The message is the first thing in the child's inbox. It triggers a cycle immediately. Tell the child what you want it to do first. Almost always include it — without a message, the child sits idle until someone sends one.
